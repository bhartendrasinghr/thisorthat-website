Every time the market goes sideways for a while, the same doubts come back. That SIPs don't make money. That you should keep your money moving instead of parking it. That India's story is finally cracking, the foreign money is leaving, and the smart returns are somewhere else. It always sounds reasonable, and it always spreads just as people begin cancelling their SIPs, close to half of them within thirty months of starting.

Thirty years of data disagrees with the entire list. A ₹10,000 monthly SIP held across three decades turned about ₹36 lakh of contributions into ₹18 to ₹22 crore. Six or seven funds did it, through every crisis and every correction, including the stretches when the news said to get out. The real question underneath the noise is simple: can you sit still long enough to let one of them work?

The people who lose money are rarely in bad funds. They are in good funds and cannot stay put. Investing gets sold as an information problem: pick the right fund, read the right returns, time the right entry. The bigger variable is how long you hold.

### Volatility is the bill

Markets consolidate and correct. After a long run-up, a flat or falling stretch is normal, the shape every equity market has held for fifty years. Each time, we assemble a fresh reason why this fall is the dangerous one: the war, the oil spike, the weak rupee, the FIIs leaving. Underneath all of it, the rupee has lost about 3.5% a year on average since 1947, through every one of those stories. Volatility is the price you are paid to accept. A market that never fell would be a market that never rewarded you for staying.

### The one sale you run from

Put 30% off on shirts and the whole city runs to the store. A correction is 30% off on equity, and the same people cancel their SIP. A fall is a discount on units. Rupee cost averaging works because you buy more when the price drops, so the fall is the engine, and quitting during it is how you stall the engine. The maths rewards whoever keeps going, and we mistake the quitting for caution.

### The silent decade

Compounding does its early work in the dark. For eight or nine years an equity SIP looks unremarkable, and the portfolio gives you nothing to boast about. Then the curve turns and the number jumps. Almost nobody waits for it. Close to half of all SIPs are cancelled within two and a half years, and the average holding period in India sits near three and a half. The wealth sits on the far side of a decade most people quit, and most of the damage happens inside that same decade. A flat stretch that makes people panic is just this decade doing its ordinary quiet work.

### You already know how to do this

Indian households already hold assets for twenty years without blinking. We do it with gold and with property. Nobody sells the family flat over two weak quarters, and nobody dumps gold in a soft year; they wait, or they buy more, because they believe the price comes back. Equity is the only one of the three we bail on, and it is the only one of the three that clears inflation. The patience already exists in the house. It just never reaches the equity folder.

### The mistake is usually yours

The fund manager is rarely the problem. When a fund disappoints, the buyer usually entered at the wrong time and judged it against the wrong benchmark. The top fund this year will not be the top fund next year, and that turnover is how the game works. The costlier habit is chasing returns, which is exactly what the noise always encourages: move your money, jump to whatever just ran up, keep circulating. Hopping between funds and hunting whoever topped the one-year chart has cost ordinary portfolios the plain market return they would have earned by sitting still. Chase the extra and you tend to lose the ordinary.

The real variable is the expectation you carry in. Expect 12% and 12% keeps you invested, with the money still doubling roughly every six years. Expect 25% and the first 12% year sends you looking elsewhere, usually right before the decade pays.

### What to actually do

Audit your own behaviour before you audit the fund. Set the expectation at 12% and let compounding stay boring. Treat a crash like a festival sale and keep the SIP running through it. Fund retirement before every other goal, because a house, a wedding, and a degree all come with a loan, and old age does not. And because money turns everyone emotional, keep one steady person beside you for twenty years whose only task is to stop you selling at the wrong moment. That role earns more than any fund they could pick.

The doubt comes back in a different costume every few years, and the data has answered it every time. The returns belong to whoever can sit through the boredom of the silent decade and let it finish.
