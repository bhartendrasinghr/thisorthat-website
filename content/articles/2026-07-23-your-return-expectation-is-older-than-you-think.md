Every time the market goes sideways for long enough, the same sentence starts circulating. The India story is finished, and whatever worked for the last twenty years has quietly stopped working.

Count how often this has happened. Sanctions after Pokhran and an Asian currency crisis arrived in the same year. Russia defaulted the year after that, then the internet bubble collapsed. In 2008, oil at $145, a rupee down a quarter, attacks in Mumbai, and a governance fraud at Satyam three months later. The Fragile Five in 2013. IL&FS and a frozen credit market in 2018, with COVID waiting behind it. A war, American rates at six percent, and $129 oil in 2022. Six separate occasions in under thirty years when informed opinion held that the country had run out of road.

All six now read as buying opportunities, though not one of them felt that way to anybody living inside it.

## The unfinished one is the only one that frightens you

A horror film you have already seen is a different object from the one you are watching for the first time. The footage has not changed and neither has the jump at the forty minute mark. What changed is that you know the ending, and knowing the ending converts fear into pattern.

Every crisis on that list has an ending attached to it now. The one you happen to be standing inside has not been assigned an ending yet, and will not be for years. So the mind quietly reclassifies 2008 as a lesson while treating the present as a threat to survival, and the gap between those two classifications has nothing to do with severity.

This is why the sixth crisis always feels like the first. Investors are not being stupid about it. Memory does something to a completed event that it simply cannot do to one still running.

## What actually happens in a flat market

Take a stretch where the index goes nowhere, which registers to the person holding it as loss. Look at what is happening underneath and the arithmetic tells a different story.

Nominal GDP keeps compounding, averaging around twelve percent a year over three decades and dipping to roughly ten in the weak patches. Earnings recover from a bad quarter and climb back to high single digits. Corporate profit as a share of GDP, once below two percent, moves up toward five. Price stands still while the earnings underneath it grow, which means valuation corrects on its own, quietly, without a single dramatic red day. Add currency depreciation and the country gets meaningfully cheaper in dollar terms while the headlines describe a collapse.

The gap between how loud a period sounds and how much it actually moved is where most investor damage happens. A flat stretch following a strong one is closer to weather than to punishment.

## The number in your head is from another country

Most investors have never audited the number itself.

The Indian equity investor carries a return expectation of fifteen to twenty percent, and that number is real. Investors earned every point of it. It was also produced by a macro regime that no longer exists.

Work backwards from the risk free rate. When equity was delivering fifteen to twenty, the government borrowed at nine percent. Inflation sat near seven and nominal GDP was growing at thirteen to fourteen. Stack an equity risk premium on a nine percent base and fifteen falls out as arithmetic, with no magic anywhere in it.

The government now borrows at six to seven and inflation has come down alongside it. India's equity risk premium has compressed, on Damodaran's own published series, because the country is measurably less risky than it used to be. A lower base carries a smaller premium on top of it, and what arrives at the end is a smaller nominal return. Twelve to thirteen percent today sits on that new foundation the way sixteen to seventeen sat on the old one, which makes the achievement underneath identical even though the printed number has shrunk.

India got safer, and a maturing market pays less for the same work. That is the entire explanation for the smaller number.

## What a stale expectation costs you

An outdated number would be harmless if it only produced disappointment. It does something considerably more expensive than that.

If you believe you are owed seventeen percent and your portfolio delivers twelve, you go looking for the missing five. There is always somebody selling it. When the bank pays six and an instrument promises sixteen, that ten point gap is a description of risk that nobody has read out loud to you. The biggest blunders in investing get made by people reaching for high yield in a low yield world, and the reach usually starts with a sense of entitlement to a number that expired years ago.

So take the correction to your yardstick before you take it to your portfolio. Recalibrate the expectation and twelve percent stops registering as underperformance. The hunt for the missing five loses its reason to exist, and a flat stretch goes back to being ordinary.

Do that once and most of the noise loses its grip on you. The market will keep producing sixth crises for as long as you are invested in it. Your job is only to notice, while it is happening, that you are describing your mood and calling it analysis.
